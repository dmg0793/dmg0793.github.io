| $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ | Probability space: sample space, $\sigma$-field, probability measure |

## 1. Sigma-Algebras

### Definition 1.1 — $\sigma$-field

Let $\Omega$ be a nonempty set. A family $\mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^\Omega$ is called a **$\sigma$-field** (or **$\sigma$-algebra**) if:

1. $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$,
2. $A \in \mathcal{F} \implies A^c \in \mathcal{F}$,
3. $A_n \in \mathcal{F}$ for $n = 1, 2, \ldots$ $\implies$ $\displaystyle\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathcal{F}$.

---

### Example 1.2 — Trivial $\sigma$-fields

Two extreme cases:

- **(a)** $\mathcal{F} = \{\varnothing, \Omega\}$ — the **smallest** $\sigma$-field on $\Omega$.
- **(b)** $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{P}(\Omega)$ — the **largest** $\sigma$-field on $\Omega$.

---

### Definition 1.3 — Generated $\sigma$-field

The **$\sigma$-field generated by $\mathcal{G}$**, denoted $\sigma(\mathcal{G})$, is the smallest $\sigma$-field containing $\mathcal{G}$:

$$
\sigma(\mathcal{G}) := \bigcap\bigl\{\mathcal{F} \subseteq 2^\Omega : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-field and } \mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\bigr\}.
$$

---

### Example 1.4 — Borel $\sigma$-field

Let $\mathcal{G} := \{(a,b] : a \le b,\ a,b \in \mathbb{R}\}$. Then $\sigma(\mathcal{G}) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$.

---

### Definition 1.5 — Borel $\sigma$-field via topology

Let $\mathcal{T}$ denote the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$ (all open sets). The **Borel $\sigma$-field** is

$$
\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) := \sigma(\mathcal{T}).
$$

---

### Remark 1.6 — Open and closed sets

$A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is **open** iff for every $x \in A$ there exist $a_x < b_x$ with $(a_x, b_x) \subseteq A$. $A$ is **closed** iff $A^c$ is open.

---

### Theorem 1.7 — Rational open intervals generate $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$

Let $\mathcal{I} := \{(a,b) : a < b,\ a,b \in \mathbb{Q}\}$. Then $\sigma(\mathcal{I}) = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$.

**Proof.**
Since $\mathcal{I} \subseteq \mathcal{T}$, we have $\sigma(\mathcal{I}) \subseteq \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$.
Conversely, any open $A \in \mathcal{T}$ can be written as a countable union $A = \bigcup_{x \in A}(p_x, q_x)$ with $p_x, q_x \in \mathbb{Q}$, so $A \in \sigma(\mathcal{I})$. Hence $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}) \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal{I})$. $\blacksquare$

---

### Definition 1.8 — Interval classes

$$
\mathcal{I}' := \{(a,b) : a,b \in \mathbb{R}\}, \qquad \mathcal{I}'' := \{[a,b] : a,b \in \mathbb{R}\}.
$$

---

### Theorem 1.9 — $\sigma(\mathcal{I}') = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$

Since $\mathcal{I} \subseteq \mathcal{I}' \subseteq \mathcal{T}$, we get $\sigma(\mathcal{I}) \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal{I}') \subseteq \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. By Theorem 1.7, $\sigma(\mathcal{I}') = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. $\blacksquare$

---

### Theorem 1.10 — $\sigma(\mathcal{I}') = \sigma(\mathcal{I}'')$

**Proof.**
$({\subseteq})$: $(a,b) = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}[a+\tfrac{1}{n}, b-\tfrac{1}{n}] \in \sigma(\mathcal{I}'')$, so $\sigma(\mathcal{I}') \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal{I}'')$.

$({\supseteq})$: $[a,b] = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}(a-\tfrac{1}{n}, b+\tfrac{1}{n}) \in \sigma(\mathcal{I}')$, so $\sigma(\mathcal{I}'') \subseteq \sigma(\mathcal{I}')$. $\blacksquare$

## 2. Probability Spaces

### Definition 2.1 — Measure

A set function $\mu : \mathcal{F} \to [0,\infty]$ on a measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a **measure** if:

1. $\mu(\varnothing) = 0$,
2. For pairwise disjoint $\{A_i\} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$: $\mu\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mu(A_i)$.

---

### Remark 2.2 — Probability measure

If $\mu(\Omega) = 1$, we call $\mu$ a **probability measure** and write $\mathbb{P}$.

---

### Definition 2.3 — Probability space

A **probability space** is a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ where $\mathbb{P}$ is a probability measure on the measurable space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$.

---

### Theorem 2.4 — Properties of $\mathbb{P}$

Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a probability space. Then:

**(a) Monotonicity:** $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow \mathbb{P}(A) \le \mathbb{P}(B)$.

**(b) Continuity from below:** $A_n \uparrow A \Rightarrow \displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\mathbb{P}(A_n) = \mathbb{P}(A)$.

**Proof.**
**(a)** Write $B = A \cup (B \setminus A)$ disjointly: $\mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(B \setminus A) \ge \mathbb{P}(A)$.

**(b)** Set $B_1 = A_1$, $B_n = A_n \setminus \bigcup_{j<n} A_j$. Then $\{B_n\}$ are disjoint, $\bigcup B_n = A$, and

$$
\mathbb{P}(A_n) = \sum_{k=1}^n \mathbb{P}(B_k) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}(B_k) = \mathbb{P}(A). \quad\blacksquare
$$

---

### Remark 2.5 — Continuity from above

$A_n \downarrow A \Rightarrow \displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\mathbb{P}(A_n) = \mathbb{P}(A)$.

## 3. Pi-systems and Lambda-systems

### Definition 3.1 — $\pi$-system

A nonempty collection $\mathcal{P}$ is a **$\pi$-system** if it is closed under finite intersections:
$$A, B \in \mathcal{P} \implies A \cap B \in \mathcal{P}.$$

---

### Definition 3.2 — $\lambda$-system

A collection $\mathcal{L}$ is a **$\lambda$-system** if:

1. $\Omega \in \mathcal{L}$,
2. $A \in \mathcal{L} \implies A^c \in \mathcal{L}$,
3. $\{A_i\}$ pairwise disjoint in $\mathcal{L}$ $\implies \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i \in \mathcal{L}$.

---

### Theorem 3.3 — Characterization of $\sigma$-fields

$\mathcal{F}$ is both a $\pi$-system and a $\lambda$-system **if and only if** $\mathcal{F}$ is a $\sigma$-field.

**Proof.**
$(\Leftarrow)$ Clear.

$(\Rightarrow)$ For any sequence $\{A_i\} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, set $B_i = A_i \setminus \bigcup_{j < i} A_j$. The $B_i$ are disjoint elements of $\mathcal{F}$, and $\bigcup A_i = \bigcup B_i \in \mathcal{F}$ by the $\lambda$-system property. $\blacksquare$
